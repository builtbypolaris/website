Every business needs a website. You've heard that a hundred times. But the advice usually stops there, leaving you to figure out which platform to use, how much to spend, and what your site actually needs to do. This guide cuts through the noise and gives you a practical answer.

Whether you're starting from scratch or questioning whether your current site is doing its job, here's what a small business website actually needs to work.

## Why your small business website matters more than you think

Your website is often the first place a potential customer checks you out, before calling, before visiting, before trusting you with their money. It works around the clock, answering questions and building (or breaking) credibility while you're doing everything else that running a business requires.

That sounds obvious, but the implications are easy to miss. A website that loads slowly, looks broken on mobile, or makes it hard to figure out what you do will quietly cost you customers without you ever knowing. They don't call to complain. They just leave.

<a href="https://web.dev/vitals/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Google's Core Web Vitals</a> (the metrics that measure real-world user experience) directly influence how your site ranks in search results. A slow or unstable site doesn't just frustrate visitors; it gets penalized in rankings too. The bar for "good enough" has moved up, and a small business website needs to clear it.

The good news: you don't need an expensive or complicated site to compete. You need a clear, functional one.

## What your small business website actually needs

Before picking a platform or calling a developer, get clear on what your site needs to do. Most small business websites serve a simple purpose: explain what you offer, build enough trust that someone wants to contact you, and make it easy for them to do so.

<img src="https://hginwqcxibraaljphcej.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/blog-images/small-business-website/illustration-2.svg" alt="Core pages every small business website needs: home, services, contact, about, and blog" />

### Core pages

Three pages will get most small businesses to 80% of what they need:

- **Home:** a clear explanation of who you are, what you do, and who it's for. Not a paragraph of mission statement. A headline a stranger can read in five seconds and understand.
- **Services or Products:** the detail page. What do you offer, what's included, and what does it cost (or at least a starting point)? Hiding pricing is a common mistake. People will leave rather than ask.
- **Contact:** a form, a phone number, an email address. Make it easy. Don't make people hunt for how to reach you.

That's the minimum viable website. Launch with these three and iterate.

### Contact and trust signals

A contact form is necessary but not sufficient. People want to know they're dealing with a real, legitimate business. Trust signals matter, and they're often overlooked:

- A physical address (even if you work remotely, a registered business address helps)
- A business email address at your domain — not a Gmail account. If you haven't set this up yet, read our guide on <a href="/insights/how-to-create-a-business-email-with-custom-domain">setting up a business email with your custom domain</a>.
- Social proof: reviews, testimonials, or logos of clients you've worked with
- An About page with a real photo and actual names

These aren't extras. For a small business, they're the difference between someone submitting a form and someone bouncing.

### Mobile-friendliness and speed

<a href="https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Learn_web_development/Extensions/Performance/why_web_performance" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Performance is a feature</a>. A site that takes four seconds to load on mobile loses a significant chunk of visitors before they see a single word. Google also uses <a href="https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/mobile-sites" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">mobile-first indexing</a>, meaning your mobile experience determines how you rank in search.

Modern website builders handle responsive design automatically, but "responsive" and "fast" aren't the same thing. Oversized images, too many plugins, and bloated templates can still make a mobile-friendly layout slow. Always test on a real device before launch, and run your site through <a href="https://pagespeed.web.dev/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Google PageSpeed Insights</a> to catch obvious issues.

If you want to go deeper on this, our guide on <a href="/insights/how-to-improve-website-loading-speed">improving website loading speed</a> covers the main levers.

## Choosing a platform for your small business website

The platform question gets more attention than it deserves. The right answer depends on three things: your technical comfort, your budget, and how much flexibility you'll need over time. Here's an honest breakdown.

### Website builders: Wix and Squarespace

<a href="https://www.wix.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Wix</a> and <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Squarespace</a> are the two dominant DIY platforms. Both are drag-and-drop editors that don't require coding. Squarespace's plans start at $16/month (billed annually) and go up to $99/month for advanced e-commerce. Wix starts around $17/month billed annually for a business plan with no Wix ads.

Both platforms are genuinely good for simple sites. Squarespace has a reputation for more polished templates and a cleaner editor. Wix offers more flexibility (and more ways to make something look inconsistent). Neither is a bad choice for a service business that needs a clean, straightforward website.

The limitations show up when you try to do something beyond the template — deep SEO customization, complex integrations, or a non-standard layout. At that point, you're fighting the platform.

### WordPress

<a href="https://wordpress.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">WordPress</a> powers over 43% of all websites on the internet. It's open-source, highly flexible, and has an enormous ecosystem of themes and plugins. The software itself is free; you pay for hosting (typically $10–$50/month depending on the provider) and any premium themes or plugins.

WordPress is the right choice if you want more control over SEO, plan to publish content regularly, or need functionality that builder platforms don't support. The trade-off is setup complexity — you'll need to choose a host, install WordPress, configure security and backups, and keep everything updated. It's manageable, but it's more overhead than a hosted builder.

### Custom development

Custom development means a developer (or team) builds your site from scratch or on a framework like <a href="https://nextjs.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Next.js</a> or <a href="https://astro.build/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Astro</a>. You get complete control over design and functionality. You also get the highest upfront cost and ongoing maintenance responsibility.

For most small businesses, custom development isn't the right starting point. It makes sense when your site needs to integrate deeply with other systems, handle complex user workflows, or perform at a scale that hosted platforms can't support. If you're evaluating this option, read our guide on <a href="/insights/how-to-choose-a-web-developer">how to choose a web developer</a> before committing.

## How much does a small business website cost?

The range is enormous — from nearly free to tens of thousands of dollars. Here's what each approach actually looks like.

<img src="https://hginwqcxibraaljphcej.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/blog-images/small-business-website/illustration-1.svg" alt="Small business website cost comparison: DIY builder vs freelancer vs agency vs custom development" />

| Approach | Upfront cost | Ongoing cost | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| DIY builder (Wix/Squarespace) | $0 – $500 | $16 – $49/month | Days to weeks |
| Freelancer | $500 – $5,000 | Hosting ~$10–30/month | 2 – 6 weeks |
| Web agency | $3,000 – $20,000 | Retainer varies | 6 – 16 weeks |
| Custom development | $10,000+ | Varies widely | 3 – 12 months |

A few things worth noting: the DIY builder cost assumes your own time has no value, which it does. A freelancer-built site usually costs less in total money over two years than you'd think, because the platform fees and your time on a builder adds up. And an agency isn't necessarily overkill — if your website is a serious revenue driver, the ROI math can work.

The question isn't just "what can I afford?" It's "what does my site need to do, and what's the real cost of it not doing that?"

## Common mistakes that sink small business websites

Good platforms and budgets don't guarantee a good site. These are the mistakes that consistently show up, regardless of how much was spent.

**Unclear headline.** The top of your home page should answer "what do you do and for whom?" in one sentence. Most small business homepages either bury this or replace it with a vague tagline that sounds meaningful but says nothing. Test yours by showing it to someone who doesn't know your business and asking them to explain what you do.

**No clear call to action.** Every page should tell visitors what to do next. "Contact us," "Get a quote," "Book a call" — pick one and make it prominent. Multiple CTAs competing for attention usually result in no action at all.

**Stock photos of strangers.** If your About page has photos of people no one on your team has ever met, it undermines the trust you're trying to build. Real photos of real people performing real work convert better. They also differentiate you from every other business using the same Unsplash library.

**Not connecting to Google.** If you haven't set up <a href="https://search.google.com/search-console" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Google Search Console</a> and <a href="https://analytics.google.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Google Analytics</a>, you have no idea who's visiting your site or where they're coming from. You can't improve what you're not measuring. Our guide on <a href="/insights/how-to-get-your-business-on-google">getting your business on Google</a> covers the basics.

**Skipping the domain email.** A contact email at your custom domain (you@yourbusiness.com) is a small detail that signals professionalism. A Gmail address does the opposite.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q: How much does a small business website cost to build?**
A: It depends on who builds it and what it needs to do. A DIY builder like Wix or Squarespace runs $16–$39 per month plus a few hours of your time. Hiring a freelancer typically costs $500–$5,000 for a complete project. A web agency usually starts around $3,000 and can go much higher depending on scope. Custom development is the most expensive option and rarely necessary for small businesses starting out.

**Q: What pages does a small business website need?**
A: Start with three: Home, Services (or Products), and Contact. An About page is strongly recommended because it builds trust and helps people understand who they're working with. A blog or FAQ section is optional at launch but valuable over time for SEO and answering common customer questions.

**Q: Which platform is best for a small business website?**
A: There's no universal answer — it depends on your budget, technical comfort, and how much flexibility you need. Wix and Squarespace are good for simple sites without a developer. WordPress suits businesses that want more control and are comfortable managing plugins. If your site needs custom features or heavy integrations, a developer-built solution is worth the investment.

**Q: Can I build a small business website myself?**
A: Yes, and for simple sites it's a reasonable choice. Platforms like Wix and Squarespace are built for non-developers and you can get something live in a day or two. The trade-off is design flexibility — template-based sites can look generic, and some platforms charge for features you'll eventually need (e-commerce, custom domains, removing ads). If your site is central to your business, getting professional help usually pays off.

**Q: How long does it take to build a small business website?**
A: A DIY builder site can be live in days. A freelancer project typically takes 2–6 weeks depending on the scope and how quickly you provide content. An agency project runs 6–16 weeks, sometimes longer if there are approval layers. The biggest delay in most projects isn't the build — it's waiting on copy, photos, and stakeholder sign-off from the client's side.

**Q: Does a small business website need to be mobile-friendly?**
A: Absolutely. Most website traffic now comes from mobile devices, and Google uses mobile-first indexing — meaning it evaluates the mobile version of your site for search rankings. A site that isn't mobile-friendly will both frustrate visitors and rank lower in search results. Most modern platforms and templates handle this automatically, but always check on an actual phone before going live.
