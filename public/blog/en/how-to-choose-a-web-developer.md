Picking a web developer feels like it should be straightforward. You need a website, they build websites. But once you start looking, you'll find hundreds of options ranging from solo freelancers to full-service agencies, all promising great results at wildly different price points. Making the wrong choice doesn't just cost money, it costs time you won't get back.

This guide breaks down how to choose a web developer who actually delivers. No fluff, no vague advice. Just the specific things to look for, the questions to ask, and the red flags that should make you walk away.

## Why choosing a web developer is harder than it looks

The web development market has no barrier to entry. Anyone with a laptop and a Squarespace account can call themselves a developer. That means the gap between the best and worst options is enormous, and it's not always obvious from the outside which is which.

Most businesses start by Googling "web developer near me" or asking around on social media. You get a flood of responses, portfolios that all look decent on the surface, and pricing that ranges from $500 to $50,000 for seemingly the same thing. The confusion isn't your fault. It's a genuinely messy market.

The real challenge is that what makes a developer good isn't visible in their marketing. It's in how they communicate, how they handle problems mid-project, and whether the site they build actually works for your business after launch. You won't find that out from a portfolio alone.

## Define what you need before reaching out

Before you contact a single developer, get clear on what you're actually looking for. This step saves you from wasting time on conversations that go nowhere.

### Type of project

Are you building from scratch, redesigning an existing site, or adding features to what you already have? A simple brochure site is a completely different project from an e-commerce store with payment processing and inventory management. The developer who's great at one might not be the right fit for the other.

### Budget range

<a href="https://wtmarketing.com/blog/choose-web-designdevelopment-company-six-key-questions-people-dont-ask/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">According to industry data</a>, small-to-mid-sized business websites typically cost $15,000 to $25,000. Larger sites with integrations run $25,000 to $50,000 or more. If you don't have a budget in mind, you'll struggle to evaluate whether a quote is reasonable.

You don't need an exact number, but having a range helps. It also lets you filter out developers who are clearly outside your bracket, either too expensive or suspiciously cheap.

### Timeline

Most web projects span 12 to 20 weeks. If you need something live in three weeks, that limits your options significantly. Be honest about your deadline and ask whether it's realistic for the scope you're describing.

## What to look for in a web developer's portfolio

A portfolio tells you what a developer can build, but you have to know how to read it properly.

### Check live websites, not screenshots

As <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/how-choose-reliable-web-developer-even-youre-tech-savvy-bill-lyons-udtje" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">web consultant Bill Lyons puts it</a>, if you're looking at a portfolio and you're not impressed, move on, because "you're likely seeing their best work." But don't stop at how it looks. Click through the actual live sites. Check if they load fast, work on mobile, and feel intuitive to navigate.

A beautiful screenshot doesn't tell you whether the site runs slowly, breaks on certain browsers, or has navigation that confuses real visitors.

### Look for projects similar to yours

A developer who's built twenty restaurant websites might not be the best choice for your logistics SaaS. Look for work that matches your industry, your complexity level, or at least the type of functionality you need. If they've solved similar problems before, they'll solve yours faster and with fewer surprises.

### Pay attention to results, not just visuals

If a developer can point to measurable outcomes (faster load times, increased conversions, better search rankings) that's more valuable than pixel-perfect design alone. Ask them about the results their work has achieved, not just how it looked at launch.

## Questions to ask before you sign anything

The questions you ask during the evaluation process reveal more about a developer than their portfolio ever will. <a href="https://www.orbitmedia.com/blog/5-questions-to-ask-when-choosing-a-web-design-firm/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Orbit Media's guide on choosing a web design firm</a> suggests 20 questions worth asking. Here are the ones that matter most.

### Technical and process questions

Start with the basics that reveal how they actually work:

- **How do you get to know our business?** Look for developers who conduct stakeholder interviews and focus on your messaging and goals, not just aesthetics.
- **What platform do you recommend, and why?** The answer should be specific to your needs, not just "we use WordPress for everything."
- **How do you approach SEO during the build?** SEO should be integrated from the sitemap phase onward, not bolted on at the end.
- **How do you handle scope changes?** Good developers document changes in writing with clear cost implications. No surprise invoices.

### Ownership and access questions

This one is critical. <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/how-choose-reliable-web-developer-even-youre-tech-savvy-bill-lyons-udtje" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Bill Lyons warns</a> that some developers "can hold your site hostage" if you don't clarify ownership upfront. Make sure you'll own the site, the content, the domain, and that you'll have full admin access to hosting and the backend.

Ask directly:

- **Who owns the finished website and all its content?**
- **Will I have admin access to the hosting, domain, and CMS?**
- **Can I take the site to a different developer later if I need to?**

If the answer to any of these is vague, that's your cue to keep looking.

### Post-launch support

A website isn't done the day it launches. Ask about maintenance plans, security updates, and how they handle bug fixes. Some developers offer ongoing retainers. Others deliver the project and disappear. Know which type you're hiring.

## Red flags that should make you walk away

Not every warning sign is obvious. Here are the ones that consistently predict problems.

**No live portfolio.** If a developer only shows screenshots or mockups, they might not have real client work to show. That's a risk you don't need to take.

**Suspiciously low pricing.** As the saying goes, cheap work isn't good and good work isn't cheap. A $2,000 quote for a custom business website usually means template work with minimal customization, or a developer who'll cut corners to make the math work.

**Poor communication early on.** If they're slow to respond, vague in their answers, or send emails full of typos during the sales process (when they're supposed to be impressing you), imagine how it'll be mid-project when they already have your deposit.

**Refuses video calls.** Building a website requires close collaboration. A developer who won't get on a call is hard to work with and harder to trust. As <a href="https://dev.to/ingosteinke/how-to-find-a-good-developer-b34" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">developer Ingo Steinke notes</a>, "collaboration and communication are the most crucial priorities for a successful web project."

**Treats every feature as an expensive add-on.** Contact forms, mobile responsiveness, basic SEO setup: these are standard. If a developer charges extra for each of these individually, the final bill will look nothing like the original quote.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q: What's the most important factor when choosing a web developer?**
A: Communication. A developer who listens, asks good questions about your business, and explains their process clearly will almost always deliver a better result than someone with a flashier portfolio but poor communication skills.

**Q: Should I choose a freelance web developer or an agency?**
A: It depends on your project scope and budget. Freelancers work well for smaller projects and are usually more affordable. Agencies bring multiple specialists and can handle complex builds with integrations, but they cost more. Either way, vet them the same way.

**Q: How much should I expect to pay when choosing a web developer?**
A: For a small-to-mid-sized business website, expect to invest $15,000 to $25,000. Larger sites with CRM integrations or custom features typically run $25,000 to $50,000 or more. Be cautious of quotes that seem too low, as they often come with hidden trade-offs.

**Q: What red flags should I watch for when choosing a web developer?**
A: Watch out for no live portfolio (only screenshots), poor or slow communication, reluctance to do video calls, and vague answers about who owns the finished website. If they can't clearly explain their process or timeline, that's also a problem.

**Q: How long does a web development project typically take?**
A: Most projects span 12 to 20 weeks depending on complexity. A simple brochure site sits on the shorter end, while e-commerce or sites with custom integrations take longer. Any developer promising a full custom build in two weeks should raise concerns.
