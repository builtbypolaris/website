If you're still using a free Gmail or Yahoo address for your business, you're sending a signal you might not intend. When a potential client gets an email from you@gmail.com instead of you@yourbusiness.com, it raises a question about how serious the operation is. Fair or not, that's how people read it.

Setting up email on your own domain isn't complicated or expensive. It takes about 30 minutes and costs as little as $1 per user per month. Here's how to set up email for your business domain, step by step.

## Why a business domain email matters

Beyond looking professional, a domain email gives you practical advantages that free email can't match.

**Credibility.** Customers and partners take you@yourcompany.com more seriously than you@gmail.com. It signals that your business is established and invested in its presence.

**Control.** When an employee leaves, you control the account. With personal Gmail accounts, they take the inbox (and all those customer conversations) with them.

**Team scalability.** Need sales@, support@, or info@ addresses? Domain email lets you create as many addresses as you need, often included in your plan.

**Deliverability.** Emails from custom domains with proper authentication (SPF, DKIM, DMARC) are less likely to land in spam folders compared to free email services.

Three providers dominate business email. Here's how they compare:

### Google Workspace

The most popular choice for small businesses. You get Gmail with your custom domain, Google Calendar, Google Drive, and the full suite of Google apps. The interface is familiar to most people, and the spam filtering is among the best available. Starts at $7/user/month for the Business Starter plan.

### Microsoft 365

If your team already uses Outlook, Word, and Excel, Microsoft 365 keeps everything in one ecosystem. You get desktop versions of all Office apps plus 50 GB of email storage per user. Starts at $6/user/month for the Business Basic plan (web-only apps) or $12.50/user/month for Business Standard (desktop apps included).

### Zoho Mail

The budget option that still delivers. <a href="https://www.zoho.com/mail/custom-domain-email.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Zoho Mail</a> offers custom domain email starting at just $1/user/month, making it the most affordable option by a wide margin. The interface is clean and functional, though it lacks the app ecosystem of Google or Microsoft.

## How to set up email for your business domain: Step by step

The process is similar across all providers. Here's what you'll do:

### Step 1: Get a domain name

If you don't have one yet, register a domain through a registrar like <a href="https://www.namecheap.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Namecheap</a>, <a href="https://www.cloudflare.com/products/registrar/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Cloudflare Registrar</a>, or <a href="https://domains.google.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Google Domains</a>. A .com domain costs around $10-15/year. If you already have a website, you already have a domain and can skip this step.

### Step 2: Sign up with your email provider

Create an account with Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, or Zoho Mail. During signup, you'll enter your domain name and choose your first email address (typically something like admin@ or your name@).

### Step 3: Verify your domain

Your email provider needs to confirm you actually own the domain. They'll give you a TXT record to add to your domain's DNS settings. Log into your domain registrar, find the DNS settings page, and add the verification record. This usually takes a few minutes to propagate.

### Step 4: Update your MX records

MX records tell the internet where to deliver email for your domain. Your provider will give you specific MX records to add. Remove any existing MX records first, then add the new ones.

For Google Workspace, the MX records look something like:

| Priority | Mail server |
|----------|------------|
| 1 | ASPMX.L.GOOGLE.COM |
| 5 | ALT1.ASPMX.L.GOOGLE.COM |
| 5 | ALT2.ASPMX.L.GOOGLE.COM |

### Step 5: Set up email authentication

This is the step most people skip, and it's the one that matters most for deliverability. Set up three DNS records:

- **SPF** (Sender Policy Framework): tells receiving servers which servers are allowed to send email for your domain
- **DKIM** (DomainKeys Identified Mail): adds a digital signature to verify emails haven't been tampered with
- **DMARC** (Domain-based Message Authentication): tells receiving servers what to do with emails that fail SPF/DKIM checks

Your email provider will give you the exact records to add. It takes 5 minutes and dramatically improves your email deliverability.

### Step 6: Create user accounts

Add email addresses for your team. Most providers let you create group aliases too (like info@ or support@) that forward to one or more team members at no extra cost.

## Common mistakes to avoid when you set up email for your business domain

**Skipping email authentication.** Without SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records, your emails are more likely to land in spam. Set these up from day one.

**Not setting up email forwarding from old accounts.** If you've been using a free email, set up forwarding so you don't miss messages during the transition. Keep the old account active for at least 3 months.

**Creating too many shared inboxes.** It's tempting to create info@, support@, sales@, contact@, and hello@ all at once. Start with what you actually need and add more later.

**Forgetting about mobile.** Set up your business email on your phone too. Google Workspace works natively with the Gmail app, and Microsoft 365 with the Outlook app.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q: How much does it cost to set up email for your business domain?**
A: Prices range from about $1/user/month with Zoho Mail to $7/user/month with Google Workspace. Most providers include email hosting, calendar, and basic storage. The domain itself costs $10-15/year separately if you don't already have one.

**Q: Can I set up email for my business domain without a website?**
A: Yes. You only need a domain name, not a website. You can register a domain just for email and set up MX records to point to your email provider. Many businesses do this as a first step before building a website.

**Q: What's the best provider to set up email for your business domain?**
A: Google Workspace is the most popular choice for its familiar Gmail interface and strong spam filtering. Microsoft 365 is better if your team relies on Outlook and Office apps. Zoho Mail is the most affordable option for small teams.

**Q: How long does it take to set up email for your business domain?**
A: The actual setup takes 15-30 minutes. However, DNS changes (MX records) can take up to 48 hours to fully propagate. Most providers start working within a few hours though.

**Q: Will I lose emails if I set up email for my business domain and switch providers?**
A: No, as long as you migrate properly. Most providers have migration tools that import your existing emails, contacts, and calendar. The key is to keep your old account active until the migration is fully complete.
