You've decided to hire a developer for your website. You have a rough idea of what you want. So you send over a few bullet points, maybe a competitor link, and wait for a quote.

Then you get back a proposal that looks nothing like what you imagined. Or worse, three wildly different proposals from three developers, none of which seem to match your request.

The problem usually isn't the developer. It's the brief.

A website brief is the single document that shapes your entire project. When it's vague, your developer fills in the blanks with assumptions. When it's clear, you get accurate timelines, realistic budgets, and a final product that actually solves your problem.

Here's how to write a website brief for a developer that does the job properly.

## Why your website brief matters more than you think

Most people treat the brief as a formality. Something to quickly type up before getting to the "real work" of design and development. That's backwards.

Your brief is the foundation of every decision your developer makes. It determines how they structure the site, which technologies they choose, how they prioritize features, and how they quote the project.

A weak brief creates a chain reaction of problems:

- **Inaccurate quotes.** Without clear scope, developers either pad the estimate to cover unknowns or lowball it and hit you with change requests later.
- **Misaligned expectations.** You picture a sleek, minimal site. Your developer builds something feature-heavy. Neither of you is wrong. You just weren't on the same page.
- **Scope creep.** When requirements aren't documented, every new conversation introduces "just one more thing." The project balloons in cost and timeline.
- **Revision loops.** Unclear direction means more rounds of feedback, more wasted hours, and more frustration on both sides.

The 2-3 hours you spend writing a thorough brief will save you weeks of back-and-forth during the project.

## The sections every website brief for a developer should include

You don't need a 30-page document. You need a focused brief that answers the questions your developer actually has. Here are the sections that matter.

### Business overview

Give your developer context about who you are. Not your full company history, but enough to understand your market position. Include what you sell, who you sell it to, and what makes you different from competitors. This helps your developer make design and content decisions that actually fit your brand.

### The problem you're solving

This is the most important section and the one most briefs skip entirely. Why are you building (or rebuilding) this website? Be specific.

Bad: "We need a modern website."

Good: "Our current site converts at 0.8% and loads in 6 seconds on mobile. We're losing leads to competitors with faster, cleaner sites."

When your developer understands the problem, they can propose solutions you might not have considered.

### Target audience

Go beyond basic demographics. Your developer needs to understand how your audience behaves online. Do they browse on mobile or desktop? Are they comparing you against competitors? Are they ready to buy or still researching?

This information directly affects site structure, page flow, and which features to prioritize.

### Goals and success metrics

Define what a successful website looks like in measurable terms. "More traffic" isn't a goal. "Increase organic traffic by 40% in six months" is. Common goals include:

- Generating a specific number of leads per month
- Reducing bounce rate below a target percentage
- Improving page load time to under 2 seconds
- Ranking on page one for specific search terms

These metrics give your developer something concrete to optimize for.

### Site structure and features

List the pages you need and any specific functionality. For each feature, describe what it should do from the user's perspective, not how it should be built technically. Your developer will determine the best technical approach.

Common features to specify:

- Contact forms and where they should submit data
- E-commerce functionality and payment processing
- Blog or content management needs
- Integrations with CRM, email marketing, or analytics tools
- User accounts or gated content
- Multi-language support

### Design direction

Share 3-5 websites you like and explain what specifically appeals to you. "I like this site" isn't helpful. "I like how this site uses whitespace to make the content feel less overwhelming" gives your developer something to work with.

If you have brand guidelines, attach them. If you don't, at minimum provide your logo, brand colors, and any fonts you use in other materials.

### Content plan

Content delays kill web projects. Be upfront about where your content stands. Are you providing final copy, or does it need to be written? Do you have professional photography, or will you need stock images?

Your developer needs to know who's responsible for content and when it will be ready. Most timeline overruns trace back to content that wasn't prepared on time.

### Budget and timeline

Two things clients love to leave out, and two things developers need most.

Your budget doesn't have to be an exact number. A range works. But withholding it entirely just wastes everyone's time. If your budget is $3,000 and the developer's minimum project starts at $10,000, it's better to know that before either of you invests hours in proposals and meetings.

For timelines, separate your ideal launch date from your hard deadline. And be realistic. A custom website typically takes 6-12 weeks depending on complexity. If you need it in two weeks, that changes everything about how the project gets scoped.

## How to write a website brief for a developer without being technical

Here's something that trips up a lot of business owners: they think they need to speak the developer's language. You don't.

The best briefs focus on business problems, not technical solutions. You don't need to specify whether the site should use React or WordPress, or whether the database should be PostgreSQL or MySQL. That's your developer's job.

What you do need to communicate clearly:

- **What the site needs to do.** "Customers should be able to book appointments online and receive confirmation emails."
- **Who it's for.** "Our primary users are business owners aged 30-50 who find us through Google."
- **What success looks like.** "We want 20 qualified leads per month from the website."

When you describe outcomes instead of implementations, you give your developer room to propose the best solution rather than just executing your guesses.

## Mistakes that make developers cringe

After reviewing hundreds of briefs (and building websites from them), certain patterns keep showing up. Here are the ones that cause the most problems.

**Describing the solution instead of the problem.** "We want a hero section with a video background and a floating CTA button" prescribes the design before anyone has analyzed what will actually convert. Start with the problem. Let the developer propose the solution.

**Copying a competitor's site.** "We want something like [competitor URL]" sounds simple, but it ignores the fact that your competitor's site was built for their audience, their goals, and their budget. Your site should be built for yours.

**Leaving out the budget.** This forces developers to either guess or send you a generic proposal. Neither outcome helps you. Transparency about budget lets your developer scope something realistic.

**Changing the brief after kickoff.** Studies show that the vast majority of projects experience brief changes after work has started. Each change costs time and money, and it erodes the trust between you and your developer. Get alignment before development begins.

**Writing a wishlist instead of a brief.** A list of 47 features isn't a strategy. It's a shopping list. Prioritize ruthlessly. What does the site absolutely need to launch? What can wait for phase two?

## A quick checklist before you send your brief

Before you hand your brief to a developer, run through this list:

- Does the brief explain why you need this website (not just what it should look like)?
- Have you defined measurable goals?
- Is the target audience described in terms of behavior, not just demographics?
- Have you listed required features with descriptions of what each should accomplish?
- Are design references accompanied by explanations of what you like about them?
- Is content responsibility assigned with deadlines?
- Have you included a budget range?
- Is there a realistic timeline with a clear launch date?
- Have you identified a single decision-maker to avoid design-by-committee?

If you can check every box, your developer has what they need to give you an accurate proposal and build something that works.
