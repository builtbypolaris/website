You're getting traffic. People find your website through search, social media, or referrals. But when you check your inbox or CRM, the inquiries don't match the visitor count. Most of those visitors browse, maybe read a page or two, and leave without doing anything.

This is the most common frustration we hear from business owners. The website looks fine. It has all the right information. But it's not converting visitors into leads. The average website converts at just <a href="https://firstpagesage.com/reports/b2b-conversion-rates-by-industry-fc/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">2-3% across industries</a>, which means 97 out of 100 visitors leave without reaching out.

The good news: you don't need a complete redesign to fix this. Small, targeted changes to your site's structure, messaging, and technical performance can make a significant difference. Here's how to get more leads from your website, broken down into the areas that matter most. (For a deeper dive on the highest-converting page type, see our guide on <a href="/insights/how-to-create-a-landing-page-that-converts">how to create a landing page that converts</a>.)

## Make your value proposition impossible to miss

When someone lands on your website, they decide within seconds whether to stay or leave. If your headline doesn't immediately tell them what you do and why it matters to them, they're gone.

Most business websites lead with something vague like "We deliver solutions" or "Your trusted partner." These phrases say nothing. A strong value proposition answers three questions in one sentence: what do you do, who do you do it for, and what outcome can they expect?

Compare these two headlines:

- "Professional consulting services for your business needs"
- "We help e-commerce brands cut shipping costs by 30% in 90 days"

The second one works because it's specific. It names the audience, the benefit, and the timeline. Visitors instantly know if they're in the right place.

Your value proposition should sit at the top of your homepage, above the fold, in the largest text on the page. Don't bury it below a stock photo carousel or behind an animation. Make it the first thing people read.

## Write calls to action that actually drive action

A call to action (CTA) tells visitors what to do next. It sounds simple, but most websites get this wrong in one of two ways: the CTA is either invisible or too generic.

"Submit," "Learn More," and "Click Here" don't give visitors a reason to act. They're placeholders, not persuasion. Specific CTAs that describe the outcome perform dramatically better.

The data backs this up. According to <a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/personalized-calls-to-action-convert-better-data" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">HubSpot's research</a>, personalized CTAs convert 202% better than generic versions. And adding urgency to a CTA, such as a limited-time offer or deadline, can boost conversions by up to <a href="https://wisernotify.com/blog/call-to-action-stats/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">332%</a>.

Here's what good CTAs look like in practice:

- Instead of "Submit," use "Get my free quote"
- Instead of "Learn More," use "See pricing for your business"
- Instead of "Contact Us," use "Book a 15-minute call"

Beyond the text, placement matters. Your primary CTA should appear above the fold, then repeat at natural decision points as visitors scroll. Pages with a single, focused CTA convert <a href="https://www.dashclicks.com/blog/cta-best-practices" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">266% better</a> than pages with competing options. Pick one action you want visitors to take, and build the entire page around it.

## Fix your page speed before anything else

You can have the best copy and the most compelling offer on the internet, but if your page takes five seconds to load, most visitors will never see it. Speed isn't a nice-to-have. It's the foundation that everything else depends on.

The numbers are clear. Pages that load in 1 second achieve a <a href="https://www.cloudflare.com/learning/performance/more/website-performance-conversion-rates/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">39% conversion rate</a>. At 3 seconds, that drops to 29%. And once you hit 5 seconds, the bounce rate climbs to 38%. On mobile, <a href="https://www.pingdom.com/blog/page-load-time-really-affect-bounce-rate/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">53% of visitors abandon a page</a> that takes longer than 3 seconds to load.

The most common speed killers on business websites:

- **Unoptimized images.** A single uncompressed hero image can add 3-5 seconds to your load time. Use WebP format, compress everything, and set proper dimensions so the browser doesn't have to resize on the fly.
- **Too many plugins or scripts.** Every third-party script (chat widgets, analytics tools, social embeds) adds weight. Audit what you're loading and remove anything that isn't directly contributing to leads.
- **Cheap hosting.** Budget shared hosting might save you $10 a month, but if it's adding 2 seconds to every page load, it's costing you far more in lost leads.

Test your site with <a href="https://pagespeed.web.dev/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Google PageSpeed Insights</a>. Aim for a score above 90 on both mobile and desktop. If you're below 70, speed should be your first priority before you touch anything else.

## Build trust before you ask for anything

Visitors won't fill out your contact form if they don't trust you. And on the internet, trust has to be earned in seconds, not weeks. Your website needs to do the heavy lifting that a face-to-face conversation would normally handle.

Here's what builds trust on a website:

**Social proof.** Testimonials, case studies, client logos, and review scores. These work because they shift the burden of proof from you to your existing customers. A testimonial that mentions specific results ("they increased our leads by 40% in two months") is far more persuasive than a vague endorsement.

**Credentials and certifications.** Industry certifications, partnership badges, awards, and "as featured in" logos. These are shorthand for "we're legitimate."

**Transparency.** Showing your team's real faces, listing your physical address, and making your pricing structure visible (even if it's "starting from" pricing). Businesses that hide behind stock photos and generic "About Us" copy feel less trustworthy.

**Response speed indicators.** If you promise a callback, say how fast. "We respond within 2 hours" is more convincing than "We'll get back to you soon." This matters because there are <a href="https://snov.io/blog/lead-generation-statistics/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">9x more chances</a> of converting a lead if you follow up within 5 minutes.

Place trust elements near your CTAs and forms. When a visitor is about to commit (fill out a form, click a button), that's when doubt is highest. A testimonial or security badge right next to the submit button can tip the balance.

## Reduce form friction to the minimum

Every field in your contact form is a small barrier. The more you ask for, the fewer people will complete it. This seems obvious, but most business websites still ask for phone number, company name, job title, and a message, when all they really need is a name and email address.

The rule of thumb: only ask for information you need to take the next step. If the next step is sending a quote, you might need their email and a brief description of what they need. If the next step is scheduling a call, you just need a name and contact method.

Beyond reducing fields, there are a few form design principles that increase completions:

- **Use inline validation.** Show green checkmarks as fields are filled in correctly, rather than waiting until submission to flag errors.
- **Break long forms into steps.** If you genuinely need a lot of information, use a multi-step form with a progress bar. People are more likely to finish something they've already started.
- **Offer alternatives.** Not everyone wants to fill out a form. A WhatsApp link, a phone number, or a calendar booking widget gives visitors other ways to reach you. The easier you make it to get in touch, the more leads you'll capture.
- **Write a clear form heading.** "Get a free quote in 24 hours" tells the visitor what they'll get and when. "Contact us" tells them nothing.

## Use content to attract and qualify leads

Your website shouldn't just wait for visitors to arrive. It should actively pull them in through search engines with content that answers the questions your potential customers are already asking.

Blog posts, guides, and FAQ pages serve two purposes. First, they bring in organic traffic from people searching for solutions to problems you solve. Second, they pre-qualify those visitors by giving them enough information to know whether you're the right fit.

The most effective content for lead generation isn't thought leadership or company news. It's practical, problem-solving content that directly relates to your services. If you're a web developer, a post about <a href="/insights/how-to-improve-website-loading-speed">how to improve website loading speed</a> attracts exactly the kind of person who might hire you.

Each piece of content should include a relevant CTA. If someone reads an article about improving their website speed, offer them a free speed audit. Match the offer to the content so it feels like a natural next step, not a sales pitch.

This approach compounds over time. A single well-written blog post can generate leads for years through organic search, unlike paid ads that stop the moment you turn off the budget.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q: How many leads should my website generate per month?**
A: It depends on your industry and traffic volume. The average website conversion rate sits between 2-3%. So if you get 1,000 visitors a month, you should expect 20-30 leads. If you're below that, there's likely a structural problem with your site that's costing you inquiries.

**Q: What's the fastest way to get more leads from your website?**
A: Start with your calls to action. Make sure every page has a clear, visible CTA that tells visitors exactly what to do next. Personalized CTAs convert 202% better than generic ones. This single change often produces the biggest immediate lift.

**Q: Do I need a separate landing page to get more leads from your website?**
A: Not always, but dedicated landing pages typically convert better than general pages because they focus on one offer with zero distractions. If you're running ads or email campaigns, a landing page will almost always outperform sending traffic to your homepage.

**Q: How does page speed affect my ability to get more leads from your website?**
A: Pages that load in 1 second convert at 39%, while 3-second pages drop to 29%. And 53% of mobile visitors leave entirely if a page takes longer than 3 seconds. Speed is one of the most overlooked lead generation factors.

**Q: Should I use pop-ups to get more leads from your website?**
A: Exit-intent pop-ups can work well when they offer genuine value, like a discount or free resource. But intrusive pop-ups that fire immediately tend to annoy visitors and increase bounce rates. Time them carefully and make sure the offer is worth the interruption.
