You've probably seen the advice a hundred times: "every business needs a website." That's true, but it skips the harder question. What kind of website? How much should it cost? And which of the dozens of platforms actually makes sense for your situation?

If you're figuring out how to build a website for small business, the real challenge isn't the technical part. It's making the right decisions before you start building so you don't waste money on features you don't need or a platform that doesn't fit.

This guide walks through the whole process, from defining what your site needs to do, to picking the right tool, to getting it live.

## Figure out what your website needs to do

Before you touch any website builder, spend 30 minutes writing down what your site actually needs to accomplish. This one step prevents the most common (and expensive) mistake: building the wrong thing.

### Define your goals first

Not every small business needs the same type of website. A restaurant needs an online menu and reservation link. A consulting firm needs case studies and a contact form. A retail shop needs a full ecommerce setup. Your goals determine everything that follows.

Ask yourself these questions:

- Are you selling products online, or do sales happen in person?
- Do customers need to book appointments?
- Is the site mostly for information, or does it need to process transactions?
- Do you want to generate leads through content (blogging, SEO)?

The answers tell you whether you need a simple information site, an online store, or something more custom.

### Match your site type to your budget

The type of website you need directly affects what you'll spend. Here's a realistic breakdown:

<img src="https://hginwqcxibraaljphcej.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/blog-images/how-to-build-a-website-for-small-business/illustration-0.svg" alt="Three types of small business websites compared by features and monthly cost" />

An information site with 5 to 10 pages can run on a free or low-cost plan. An online store needs payment processing and product management, which pushes you into the $19 to $49/month range. Fully custom builds (think booking systems, client portals, or automation) typically require an agency or developer.

## Pick the right platform to build a website for small business

The platform you choose locks you in for a while, so it's worth getting this right. Here's how the main options compare for small businesses.

### WordPress.com

<a href="https://wordpress.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">WordPress.com</a> is the hosted version of WordPress, and it's one of the most flexible options. Their free plan gives you 1 GB of storage and unlimited pages. The Personal plan ($4/month on a 2-year term) removes ads and adds a free domain. The Business plan ($25/month on a 2-year term) unlocks plugins, SFTP access, and staging environments.

WordPress.com works well if you want a content-heavy site (blog, portfolio, resource library) and might need advanced features later. The learning curve is steeper than drag-and-drop builders, but the flexibility is worth it for many businesses.

### Shopify

If you're selling physical or digital products, <a href="https://www.shopify.com/pricing" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Shopify</a> is purpose-built for that. Their Basic plan costs $19/month (billed yearly) and includes everything you need to start selling: product pages, a shopping cart, payment processing, and up to 10 inventory locations. The Grow plan at $49/month adds 5 staff accounts and lower transaction fees.

Shopify handles hosting, security, and payment compliance, which is a big deal if you don't want to manage that yourself. The trade-off is that it's less flexible for non-ecommerce content.

### Squarespace

<a href="https://www.squarespace.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Squarespace</a> is known for its design quality. If your business relies on visual presentation (photography, restaurants, design studios), Squarespace templates tend to look more polished out of the box than most competitors. Plans typically range from $16 to $33/month depending on the features you need.

The downside: customization is more limited than WordPress, and you're locked into their ecosystem. If your needs are primarily visual and you want something that looks great without touching code, it's a strong pick.

### Wix

<a href="https://www.wix.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Wix</a> offers a free plan and a drag-and-drop editor that's genuinely easy to use. It's a solid option if you want to build something yourself quickly without a steep learning curve. Their AI site generator can create a basic site from a few prompts.

Wix works best for simple sites (under 20 pages) where you want creative control without coding. For larger or more complex projects, you may hit limitations with performance and flexibility.

| Feature | WordPress.com | Shopify | Squarespace | Wix |

## Pages every small business website needs

You don't need 30 pages. Most small businesses need 5 to 7 core pages done well. Here's what to prioritize, based on <a href="https://business.gov.au/online-and-digital/business-website/set-up-a-business-website" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">recommendations from business.gov.au</a> and common industry practice.

### Homepage

Your homepage has about 3 seconds to communicate what you do and who you serve. Include a clear headline, a brief description of your services, and one obvious call to action (book a call, view products, get a quote). Don't clutter it with everything your business has ever done.

### About page

People buy from people. Your about page should explain who you are, what you believe in, and why you started this business. Keep it genuine. Stock photos of handshakes don't build trust, but a real photo of your team does.

### Services or products page

Detail what you offer with enough information for someone to make a decision. If you sell services, include what's involved, who it's for, and (if possible) pricing. If you sell products, Shopify or WooCommerce will structure this for you.

### Contact page

Make it easy to reach you. Include a contact form, your email, phone number, business hours, and a Google Maps embed if you have a physical location. Don't make people hunt for your phone number.

### Blog or resources

This is optional but valuable for SEO. A blog with useful content targeted at your customers' questions helps you rank in Google over time. Even one post per month adds up. As <a href="https://squareup.com/us/en/the-bottom-line/starting-your-business/how-to-set-up-a-business-website" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Square's guide</a> recommends, focus your content on answering the questions your customers actually ask.

## How to build a website for small business step by step

Here's the practical sequence once you've picked your platform.

### Step 1: Register your domain

Your domain name is your online address. Keep it short, easy to spell, and connected to your business name. Avoid hyphens, numbers, or clever abbreviations that people won't remember. Most platforms include a free domain for the first year with paid plans. If you're buying separately, expect to pay $10 to $15/year through a registrar like Namecheap or Google Domains.

### Step 2: Set up your platform and pick a template

Every builder has templates organized by industry. Pick one that's close to what you want, then customize from there. Don't start from a blank canvas unless you have design experience. Most small business owners save hours by starting with a template and adjusting the colors, fonts, and images to match their brand.

### Step 3: Build your core pages

Start with the five pages above. Write your content in a separate document first (Google Docs works fine), then paste it into the builder. This keeps you focused on the words rather than getting distracted by layout tweaks.

A few content tips that make a real difference:

- Write in short paragraphs (3 to 5 sentences)
- Use headings to break up sections
- Include one clear call to action per page
- Add real photos of your business, team, or products

### Step 4: Set up basic SEO

SEO doesn't need to be complicated at this stage. Cover the basics:

- Write a unique title tag and meta description for each page
- Use descriptive URLs (yourbusiness.com/services, not yourbusiness.com/page-3)
- Add alt text to all images
- Claim your <a href="https://business.google.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Google Business Profile</a> and link it to your website

Mobile devices account for over half of all internet traffic, so test your site on your phone before launch. Every platform listed above is mobile-responsive by default, but check that nothing looks broken on a smaller screen.

### Step 5: Test, launch, and set up analytics

Before going live, click through every page. Check that forms work, links aren't broken, and images load properly. Test on at least two browsers (Chrome and Safari cover most users). Then install <a href="https://analytics.google.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Google Analytics</a> so you can track what's working once you're live.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q: What's the cheapest way to build a website for small business?**
A: WordPress.com offers a free plan with basic features, and their Personal plan starts at $4/month (on a 2-year term). Wix also has a free tier. For most small businesses, expect to spend $9 to $18/month on a platform that includes hosting, a domain, and enough customization to look professional.

**Q: Do I need a developer to build a website for small business?**
A: Not necessarily. Website builders like Wix, Squarespace, and WordPress.com let you drag and drop your way to a finished site. But if you need custom features (booking systems, app integrations, automation), working with a developer or agency can save you time and deliver a better result.

**Q: How long does it take to build a website for small business?**
A: With a website builder, you can have a basic site live in a weekend. A more polished site with custom content and SEO typically takes 2 to 4 weeks. A fully custom build with an agency usually runs 4 to 8 weeks depending on complexity.

**Q: Which platform is best to build a website for small business in 2026?**
A: It depends on what you're building. WordPress.com is the most flexible and cost-effective for content-heavy sites. Shopify is the go-to for online stores (starting at $19/month). Squarespace offers the best templates for design-focused businesses. There's no single best option for everyone.

**Q: What pages should I include when I build a website for small business?**
A: At minimum, you need a homepage, an about page, a services or products page, and a contact page. Most businesses also benefit from a testimonials or reviews section, an FAQ page, and a blog for SEO. Don't build pages you won't maintain.
