Your domain name is the first impression most customers will have of your business online. It's what they type into their browser, what shows up in search results, and what they tell friends when recommending you. Get it right and it works quietly in the background for years. Get it wrong and you'll be explaining how to spell it on every phone call.

If you're trying to figure out how to choose a domain name for your business, the good news is that it's not complicated. But there are real trade-offs between cost, availability, and long-term branding that are worth thinking through before you commit.

This guide covers what actually matters when picking a domain, which TLD extensions are worth considering, and how to avoid the mistakes that make businesses rebrand six months in.

## Keep it short, simple, and easy to say out loud

The best domain names share a few qualities: they're short, they're easy to spell, and they pass the "phone test." If you can say your domain out loud and someone can type it correctly without asking you to repeat it, you're in good shape.

Here's what to aim for:

- **Under 15 characters** (excluding the TLD). Shorter names are easier to remember, faster to type, and less prone to typos.
- **No hyphens or numbers.** "best-coffee-4-u.com" might be available, but nobody will remember it. Hyphens are especially bad because people always forget them.
- **Easy to pronounce.** If you can't say it clearly in conversation, it'll cause problems. Try telling a friend your domain name and see if they can type it correctly on the first try.
- **No double letters at the join.** If your brand is "Press" and you pick pressolutions.com, people will stumble over the double S. Watch for awkward letter combinations where your name meets the TLD.

Think of your domain name as a piece of infrastructure. You'll print it on business cards, include it in email signatures, say it at networking events, and put it on invoices. Every extra syllable or unusual spelling adds friction.

## Use your brand name (or build one around the domain)

The most straightforward approach to choosing a domain name for your business is to use your actual business name. If your company is called "Riverview Consulting," then riverviewconsulting.com is the obvious first choice.

But what if it's taken? You have a few options:

- **Add a clear prefix or suffix.** Words like "get," "try," "use," or "go" work well. getriverview.com is clean and easy to remember.
- **Shorten it.** riverview.co or rvc.io can work if your audience is tech-savvy enough to recognize alternative TLDs.
- **Don't force a keyword.** riverviewconsultingservicesllc.com is technically descriptive, but it's a nightmare to type and impossible to remember.

If you're starting a brand-new business, consider doing the domain search before you finalize the name. It's much easier to adjust a business name slightly than to rebrand after you've already printed materials and set up social accounts.

Some of the most successful companies picked their name based on domain availability. The domain became the brand, not the other way around.

## Pick the right TLD extension for your business

The TLD (top-level domain) is the part after the dot: .com, .io, .ai, .co.id, and so on. Your choice here affects both perception and cost.

Here's a practical breakdown of the most relevant options:

### .com: the default for most businesses

Over 62% of funded startups still use .com, and for good reason. It's the extension people type by reflex. If someone hears your brand name, they'll try the .com first. For consumer-facing businesses, .com is still the safest bet.

Standard .com registration costs $9 to $15/year through registrars like Cloudflare or Porkbun. Premium .com names on the aftermarket can run $5,000 to $50,000 or more.

### .io and .dev: strong for tech and SaaS

If your audience is developers or tech-savvy professionals, .io and .dev carry real credibility. With over 1.1 million registrations, .io has become the standard for developer tools and B2B SaaS products.

.dev is managed by Google and enforces HTTPS by default, which signals security from day one. Expect to pay $30 to $50/year for .io and around $15 to $20/year for .dev.

### .ai: the AI era TLD

If you're building anything related to artificial intelligence or machine learning, .ai is almost expected now. It saw 375% growth from 2022 to 2023 and continues to climb. The downside is cost: renewals run $70 to $120/year, and the extension is controlled by Anguilla, which means limited registrar options.

### Country-code TLDs: .co.id, .id, and others

If your business primarily serves a specific market, a country-code TLD can actually help build trust with local customers. Indonesian businesses, for example, can benefit from .co.id or .id, which signal that you're a legitimate operation serving that market.

Country-code TLDs typically cost $10 to $30/year, though requirements vary. Some require a local business registration or address.

## Watch out for hidden costs and renewal traps

One of the biggest surprises for first-time domain buyers is the gap between promotional pricing and what you'll actually pay long term. Understanding these costs is a critical part of learning how to choose a domain name for your business.

Here's what to watch for:

- **Renewal price increases.** Many registrars advertise .com domains at $1 to $3 for the first year, then charge $15 to $23/year on renewal. According to ICANN, more than 70% of registrar revenue comes from renewals and add-ons, not first-year sales.
- **WHOIS privacy fees.** Some registrars charge $10 to $15/year for WHOIS privacy protection. Others (Cloudflare, Porkbun, Namecheap, Spaceship) include it free. Never pay for privacy protection in 2026.
- **Premium domain markups.** Short, dictionary-word domains often carry premium pricing even if they haven't been registered before. A registrar might show a domain as "available" at $2,000/year instead of $12/year.

The best approach is to calculate the 5-year cost of ownership before committing. A .ai domain at $100/year costs $500 over five years. That same budget could get you a solid .com on the aftermarket that you'd own for $12/year going forward.

**Best-value registrars in 2026:**

- **Cloudflare Registrar**: at-cost pricing with zero markup ($10.46/year for .com), but you need to use their DNS
- **Porkbun**: consistently low prices with free WHOIS privacy and a clean interface
- **Spaceship**: $8.88 first year, $9.80 renewal for .com, strong value across the board
- **Namecheap**: competitive pricing with a large TLD selection and free privacy

## Protect your domain investment

Once you've chosen a domain name for your business, take a few extra steps to protect it:

**Register for multiple years.** Locking in 2 to 3 years upfront protects you from price increases and ensures you don't accidentally let it expire. An expired domain can be snatched up by domain squatters within hours.

**Grab the common variations.** If you register yourbrand.com, also grab yourbrand.co and any country-code TLD relevant to your market. Redirect them all to your primary domain. This prevents competitors or squatters from registering confusingly similar names.

**Enable auto-renewal and domain lock.** Most registrars offer both features. Auto-renewal prevents accidental expiration. Domain lock prevents unauthorized transfers. Both should be on by default.

**Use a dedicated email for your registrar account.** Don't tie your domain management to a personal Gmail that might get compromised. Use a business email, enable two-factor authentication, and keep your recovery information current.

## Frequently asked questions

### How do I choose a domain name for my business if the .com is taken?

Try a slight variation of your business name, like adding "get," "try," or "use" before it (e.g., getpolaris.com). You can also consider alternative TLDs like .co, .io, or a country-code extension like .co.id if you serve a specific market. Avoid hyphens or numbers as workarounds since they're hard to remember and easy to mistype.

### Does my domain name affect SEO when I choose a domain name for my business?

The domain name itself has minimal direct impact on search rankings. Google has said that TLD choice (.com vs .io vs .dev) doesn't influence rankings. What matters more is the content on your site, your backlink profile, and technical SEO. That said, a memorable domain gets more direct traffic and brand searches, which indirectly helps SEO.

### How much does it cost to choose and register a domain name for your business?

Standard .com domains cost $9 to $15 per year through registrars like Cloudflare, Porkbun, or Namecheap. Newer extensions like .io run $30 to $50/year, and .ai domains cost $70 to $120/year. Watch out for first-year promotional pricing that jumps 30 to 120 percent at renewal.

### Should I choose a domain name for my business that includes a keyword?

It can help with brand clarity (e.g., smithplumbing.com tells visitors exactly what you do), but it's not required. Brandable names like "stripe.com" or "notion.so" work just as well. Prioritize something short and easy to say out loud over stuffing keywords into your domain.

### How do I choose a domain name for my business that works long term?

Pick a name broad enough to cover future growth. If you sell shoes now but might expand into accessories later, "sarahsshoes.com" will feel limiting. Also register common misspellings and the main TLD variations (.com, .co) to protect your brand as it grows.
